Play-Eat-Walk Local! in Richmond, VA:
The Proximity of Cafes and Restaurants to Parks

By Ian Spangler

Richmond, the capital city of Virginia, seems to become more popular with each passing year. Its frequent placement on "Top 10" listicles, as well as a recent proliferation of restaurants and breweries, certainly suggests that it has something to offer. But how do such amenities fare when analyzed in relation to other desirable urban features – for example, parks? The above map attempts to answer the question how accessible different cafes and restaurants are based on their distance from parks in Richmond, VA, and the following points were considered during analysis.

RVA is no stranger to public green space – there are nearly 100 parks in the city, many of which are tiny triangle parks. As such, for this analysis, only parks larger than 1 acre (43,560 sq ft) were selected. Additionally, while The Institute of Environmental Recreation initially requested amenities within one mile of parks, Richmond's parks proved abundant enough to reduce that distance by half (2,640 ft). Half a mile might seem too small, but when set at 1 full mile, the buffers covered nearly the whole city! In conducting the actual analysis, a buffer of 2,640 ft was created on all parks within the City of Richmond's formal boundary, resulting in 48 buffers on different parks (see Figure 1). This buffer was used to select cafes and restaurants within 1/2 mile of a park.

In examining these maps, we can discern a few points about the accessibility of amenities to parks in RVA. First, there are significant clusters of cafes and restaurants on the Cary/Main Street corridor and along Broad Street (see Figure 2). This is unsurprising, considering that those are both hotspots of gentrification as well as collegiate activity for students of Virginia Commonwealth University. Perhaps a more interesting observation is that there are functionally no park-accessible amenities south of the (just heinously symbolized) James River. But this analysis has many more limitations than it does illuminations. For one, mere Euclidean distance is not sufficient to analyze accessibility. A more sophisticated cost-distance analysis that considered how walkable an amenity is, rather than it's distance from a park as the crow flies, would help to better answer The Institute's question. Additionally, the classifications downloaded from OpenStreetMap need more examination to confirm that they are indeed the kinds of "cafes" or "restaurants" that the Play-Eat-Walk Local! initiative is interested in identifying. For example, while this analysis considered Dunkin' Donuts and McDonald's cafes and restaurants, it remains unclear whether those are the kinds of establishments that would be considered "local." Finally, the map is relatively misleading: while it does indeed show us that there are a total of 90 cafes or restaurants within 1/2 mile walking distance of parks in the city, it neglects to mention that there were only 99 such amenities in the city to begin with! Put another way, the buffers did nearly nothing at all in terms of differentiating an amenity that was park-accessible from one that simply existed. If anybody still wants to check out the GeoJSON, though, they can find it here.
